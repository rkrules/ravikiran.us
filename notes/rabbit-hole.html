Rabbit Hole

April 23, 2020
On Easter sunday, I decided to look more into a post that I had seen on Reddit that had intrigued me. It was someone being able to code a video conferencing solution on web, and wouldn't need any additional plugin or software. When I dug more into it, realized it is made possible by WebRTC. Then I followed the quest .. who use WebRTC and if there are other open source software. 8x8is one such company that offers enterprise solutions. Jitsi was another, open source solution, and has been acquired by 8x8. Both solutions offer free unlimited duration conference, screen sharing and also offers phone call-ins. Both services don't require creating accounts even to host calls. (Zoom's free plan during covid has removed phone call-ins, and free plan restricts the meeting for 40 minutes for more than 2 people. WebEx has offered unlimited duration in the free plan during the covid crisis).  One can host Jitsi on their server or use Jitsi's open source to create their own client. Discord, a popular chatting app for gamers ( well, it's used by small companies and startups too) uses the same WebRTC technology. Well two of the largest apps that use WebRTC are of course Google (who created and open sourced WebRTC) Hangout which is one of the chat apps we all wow'd before Google+ ruined everything nice, and Facebook messenger. I got curious about quality of Jitsi vs let's say, Zoom - as Zoom has been in conversation everywhere. Lo and behold, there is a post comparing the quality with congestion testing. Though on desktop, any software or plugin is not needed, on iPhone, apps are needed. And this is because, Apple hasn't supported WebRTC, and thus any browser on Apple wouldn't be supporting WebRTC. Which led me to look up browser engine history and support,  mobile support. On Android, Firefox and Chrome do support WebRTC. I then tried out the 8x8 and Jitsi apps. Both had impressive features. On Jitsi, unless meetings are protected by passwords, one just needs room name to enter a meeting. Just to experiment, I entered a room called 'ravi' and to my utter surprise, there was a meeting happening. I quit in panic. But I couldn't just believe the odds, on an Easter Sunday noon, on a not very well known ( maybe just to me then ?) video conferencing app, my first attempt to randomly create/enter a room succeeded, and that too with a name 'ravi'.
