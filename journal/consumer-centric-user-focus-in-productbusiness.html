Consumer centric/ User focus in Product/Business meetings

October 25, 2014
Business
These days I am counting time until when someone else explicitly raises one of the two questions that are critical to building good products. Product managers are supposed to breathe this, yet I see again and again, this fails on the leaders (most of whom are inexperienced because they didn’t grow up the ranks of Product Management). The two questions I would like to hear are
1. What is the problem we are trying to solve
2. What is the value to the end user / customer
*
There are many subsets and extensions of these questions that make sense, but at the least I would like to hear these questions. Often it is 60-70% into a meeting, if not never that I get to hear these questions. Now, being a consumer facing company in a commodotized market it is often difficult to represent or think of end user, but it is very essential. There is an implicit understanding of whom we are serving, but making it explicit and obvious is so important because we get lost in conversations.
In one of the recent meeting with partners (a distribution channel for our product - but who use our content to make money for them & us in a revenue sharing agreement) I again realized this. It was because I thought their discussion would at least be more end user focused because they are one step closer to the user than us, however our meeting was lost in discussion about how to make more money for both of us. It is for this reason I am not a big fan of advertising business model, but as I said in some other post, we have been thankful to ads in a huge way.
*
Relatedly, three attitudes that I don’t like, I end up observing are
1) Relying on assumptions rather than data/ willingness to test the assumption
2) Build it, they will come attitude.
3) Lack of scale
In these days when the analytics tools have become commodotized, it is still a shame that people consciously don’t think of testing something. Agreed that there is a huge development cycle either with apps and even more with hardware such as phones, yet it is no excuse not to test the hypothesis. 
On the other hand, there is ton of data that is collected, but not everyone understands how to put that to good use. 
*
“Build it” attitude is too common. And almost all the time it is based on assumptions. The other day I was in an IoT meeting which went on for hours showing off all these cool products which are either in prototype or in test phase - half the audience was awed because they didn’t realize tech can do such cool stuff. The other half was busy thinking how to insert our data or content into such products and how to sell and make money. Neither one was wondering if this is really of value to the user, whether this is going to sell, and if it did whether it had the capability to gain traction and sell in volume. 
There are so many ideas (whether in the industry or that can be put into our products) that don’t have the promise/scale that it can make an impact or it can be a differentiator. Yet people praise it, pass around emails, hold brainstorm and push for estimates, and if lucky into implementation. I strongly believe innovation is required and every new idea is interesting - but innovation and scale shouldn’t be applied to the same product. 
My proposal : separate the innovation, create a test bed, test rigorously.
Once test is successful, think of scale and $. These two should be separate teams, separate products, separate mindset.
