On optimism

September 11, 2014
Business

Today’s reading included this gem - project management lessons learned from NASA. 

http://www.nasa.gov/offices/oce/llis/imported_content/lesson_1956.html

Lot of stuff there is relevant even in today’s age in any project management, especially in software project management that I can relate to.

**

There were several points that triggered my thoughts, but I will tackle one today : that's 

engineers are born optimists

This , I guess is true in general. I was an engineer myself, and I learned a lot due to my peers during my engineering. They were all optimists - they never doubted that something can’t be done. They were mostly self taught, and attempted all questions in quizzes and exams, despite there being options to answer a smaller subset of questions.

While working, our team of engineers were such a team - who said yes to every problem or task, often without understanding that accepting those tasks would make us lose sleeps and weekends.

**

By definition, engineer solves problems. Engineers build things and fix things. I studied MBA because I wanted to solve business problems. I am now a Product Manager, because I wanted to build and fix products/broken user experiences. 

However in my product management career I have had to work with pessimistic engineers - who almost always said “no” or “it would be difficult” to many of the challenges I threw at them. Very rarely did I find engineers who were optimistic to say “yes, that can be done” attitude. In those cases, engineers were usually contract positions. 

While reading Jobs’ bio, I was jealous how he could push engg team to achieve impossible. If there is one trick I would like to learn, that would be to challenge and push engineers. 

**

While I am often discouraged by the pessimist attitude of engineers, I am equally annoyed by the optimism of certain product managers. I have come across such inexperienced product managers who tend to throw in complex requirements that are never before implemented to a project that already has a short deadline and executive’s attention. Perfect recipe for failure if there ever was one. “ I believe engineering will all come together at the end… universe will make it happen, you know” was the constant mantra until I stepped in and de-scoped everything to get an MVP out. 

**

The conclusion of this article is that if engineers are more flexible to try out, to experiment, to stretch the boundaries - they are a delight to work with for product managers. At the same time, product managers should be careful and very afraid about what all might go wrong. In addition, PMs should gauge engineering difficulty and time estimations well.

Engineers are better off erring on the side of optimism, while PMs should err on the side of pessimism.
