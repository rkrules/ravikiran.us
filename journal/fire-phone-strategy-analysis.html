Fire Phone - Strategy analysis

October 24, 2014
Business

Recently there were several speculated reports about how few (30K) Fire Phones were sold in a month of time. Given how closely I follow the industry, it was not a big surprise to me. In fact I had predicted a year ago that Amazon shouldn’t enter into phones where margins are thin, competition is high and it’s too late.

But I’m sure it was not even a surprise to any common man, most of whom would have ended up asking a question - what Fire phone ?

Then how do executives who are so entrenched in the industry, who have access to so much data, get it wrong ?

Let me take a detour here. The argument that Amazon is an ecommerce company/content company and they want as many people to use their other services falls flat when you consider that phone is sold as a separate product - not a free offering - in a competitive market. When a consumer walks into a phone shop to purchase a phone - he looks at Fire phone , it’s price (and may be it’s features) and compares it with iPhone or S5. The fact that it is sold as a product in the open market, where alternate is another product, it should distinguish itself as a product first before it can entice on content offerings. In other words, if you want to increase your content consumption by offering a device to do it, offer the device as a free offering along with your services. However I know that Kindle eReader drove lot of eBook business for Amazon - but the key to note here is that Amazon was a monopoly on 1) books 2) eBooks 3)eBook reader - in fact first mover. In fact eBook reader was the best medium to access eBooks.  And nothing applies now - not a monopoly on content, phone is not the best medium to shop on Amazon and not a first mover or monopoly on either the device or the content. With all that said, phone is going to be compared to other ‘phones’. 

Getting back, how did the execs get it wrong ?  Hubris ( Kindle Fire was a decent Android tablet in a space that lacked strong product, and captured good % of Android tablet market share) ? What happened to market research ? What happened to data based decision making - what data was there to support the whole project, the go-to market strategy including pricing ?

*

I believe we know the causes for failure 

* high competition, commoditization of phones where margins are thin

* too late to market

* hard to differentiate

But there was a way this could still have been salvaged - price point. A day before iPhone 6 was announced Fire Phone reduced prices to $0.99, before that it was priced competitively with S5, iPhone. That initial price point (forget $100 back to as a Prime membership, which I will get a minute later) was a HUGE blunder. I can’t count how many companies get pricing so terribly wrong. 

Prime membership was interesting - it meant two things:

1. The target consumer for this phone is a consumer who has already bought into the benefits of Prime

2. You are trying to sell both the benefits of phone and Prime membership in one complex deal with an offering of a Phone. For just a phone buyer, this is forcing a year of Prime. 

You can see how complicated #2 sounds even while just reading it. If #1 is true, Amazon should have just offered all existing Prime members a free phone. It sounds crazy, but 

1. It would have made it exclusive to the “Prime” cult, which would have made it (both phone and Prime club) more attractive.

2. Much better offer than offering at $200+1 year Prime and then cutting to $0+ 1 year Prime. 

#2 means two things:

1. That alone would be a HUGE signal of how poor the sales have been creating a negative impression, hurting the sales even more.

2. Amazon is offering $100 in value to acquire a phone+Prime user. That’s a huge bet which only works if phone buyer finds Prime very attractive and spends gazillion $ on Prime. However, I think, a 'phone’ buyer wouldn’t remain a Prime user after the grace period. In otherwords a loyal Prime customer would have made more of the phone and a phone customer would make of Prime.

*

Final thoughts on ecosystem:

You don’t offer multiple products and services at once and entice an user to become/convert to become a loyal 'ecosystem’ user. You sell him on ONE product/service first - you deliver so much value that he trusts you, and buys into you. You then offer other 'related’ products & services, and keep on binding him tightly to your ecosystem that he can’t escape.

Apple started with iPod. Then came iTunes. Then iPhone. And you have a customer for life.

Microsoft started with Windows. Then came MS Office. Then you have the user tied to your ecosystem.

Amazon wants to tie Prime ( a collection of disparate offerings such as free 2 day shipping, limited online audio/video collection) to a communication device such as a Phone. (The Kindle Fire tablet was atleast OK - one shops more on tablet, one watches lot of videos on tablets). See how odd that sounds ?

*

I know strategy is hindsight is 20-20. I know Amazon plays for the long haul - so hoping to benefit from this initiative after 5-7 years. 

It would be interesting to see what they do with this and how they come out of this. I have been proven right too many times , but also wrong few times - but that hasn’t stopped me from speculating.

Let me boldly predict that Amazon is going to stop selling Fire phone before one year. Or they will start selling a phablet (following footsteps of Apple) and integrate eInk displays such as Yota.
