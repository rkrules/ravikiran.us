On Google Glass

November 17, 2014
Business

A high profile keynote was interrupted, another man running onto keynote with lot of energy - crowd goes berserk. A live demo of the product is being shown to launch the product - unlike any live demo this time with professional skydivers. It’s Google Glass’s unveiling and that which gets huge press over the next few months.

I was right there in the audience - while excited for a product that worked seamlessly on a demo day (Win95 anyone), it’s huge ‘potential’. After few minutes of watching the video - I was more on the side of underwhelmed. There was no value proposition - it was pretty much left to developers who would build companion products/apps - that was fair as it was a developer conference anyways. Yet, somewhere in the back of my mind, I couldn’t see it more than a hands-free camera. I even tweeted that and got replies from my friends that I am not excited/impressed , contrary to general public. I wasn’t. 

I thought of few other use cases where hands free camera would be helpful (in lab, hospitals etc) but none convinced me that Google Glass was going to be THE device for them. It was not solving a problem, or fulfilling a need they didn’t realize they had.

*

Few months later, thanks to the company, I bought a Google Glass. Wore it for couple of days. Yet I continued to be as unimpressed. I had paid $600 to get ride of my glasses , and I didn’t want to wear. I realized half the people who are being excited for glass, had never worn one in their life. Wearing glass is painful and uncomfortable. Why would I want to put through that misery again , for evidently small benefit or no benefit ?

Let’s talk about benefits - as one of my friends (who tried couple of times) said, it helps in a quick check of time. TIME ? That’s an useless use case, no matter if she checked time for 1000 times a day. Battery was terrible - however it could have improved in final versions. Apps - maps was interesting, yet not a compelling when I can always reach for my phone. Searching and reading ? Not impressed.

Anything I thought of, I wasn’t convinced that GG was going to be make the experience better. The value difference when taken into account of inconvenience was even smaller. Even hands free camera wasn’t a smooth experience. Nothing was killer use case.

Next year I went to conference again, by this time - privacy concerns, driving with glass concerns, seemingly anti-social concerns, geek attitude, etc was all well documented. Including a widely understood term and hashtag #glasshole. I saw few people wearing it around in the conference. I asked the use cases - some didn’t even have battery charged but thought it was cool to walk around with them. What !! One person said how they use it constantly and it helps them in their labs. 

There was no news of Google Glass from the podium next year ( nor was there about Google+). 

I did receive the updated Google Glass, the one I haven’t worn once. 

Google Glass will go down in my history as one more tech prediction that I got right.

Good :

After spending all the above words to say how bad this product was, what can be good. Well there are few:

1. I loved Google for attempting something so out there, for investing money and trying out something that got a lot of people talking. Even if bad publicity, GG almost got as much press if not more and attention that any normal Apple product would get. That is saying a lot.

2. Created one of the first 'wearables’ that may or may not be defining the space for others to follow. 

3. Great support and community building, campaigns, pre-release - lot of good learning for Google.

Questions:

What was the need you were trying to address ? Problem you were trying to solve ?

Any user research / testing happened whether people would want to wear around a glass and spend time ?

Finally, any sense of what scale did you plan to reach ? It is not a mass product, and would you really want to create something that is not mass adopted , at least because you are Google.
