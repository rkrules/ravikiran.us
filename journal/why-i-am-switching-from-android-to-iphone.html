Why I am switching from Android to iPhone

I have explained my reasoning behind my switching from Android to iPhone to wife and couple of friends, but jotting it down here as Medium tells me to ‘tell a story’. There is probably a simple reason that I am switching to iPhone but as I think deeper, it is because of who I am. The reason has little, not nothing, to do with the phone’s features or company’s reputation, “cool” image but more to do with who I am as a person.
My story goes back to the time I started tinkering with personal computers. Before the ‘branded’ computers — mostly ‘laptops’, were a norm among my peers, we only used assembled computers. I bought my own hard disk, sound card, mother board, RAM, graphics card and assembled to form my computer. It was good fun, but more importantly, it was the freedom we cherished to mix and match best products in each category and put together a computer. It was teenager’s way of building a ‘house’. As time progressed I got tired of doing it, and started buying branded laptops or bought assembled computers.

A similar story followed with Linux and Open Source revolution. When most of my peers were comfortable or were happily exploring different screensavers and wallpapers, couple of us were busy formatting hard-disks to install Linux. Even though we didn’t pay for Windows OS, the “free” offer from Linux was exciting and attractive. Yes, yes, the free in Linux is actually as in speech and not as in beer, but due to some computer magazines giving away a free CD every month, and many of those carried one or the other version of Linux. When one of those CDs was not enough or not latest, directly downloading 1GB Linux from websites, often using a torrent downloader on a 128 or 256kbps dial up modem are some of the excitement next generation will never be able to comprehend. Once downloaded (of course on the windows partition), it was another task to copy it to a common drive that can also be accessed from Linux partition (or Linux boot) and to configure the boot script/install script.

Loading Linux and struggling to get the display work on old graphics card was itself another adventure. X11Config was the program if I remember right. Formatting the hard disk, changing the boot loader order, using a floppy boot loader, then configuring display, downloading appropriate drivers for modem, sound card, downloading a browser + an email client all were adventures and experiments that ended in equal amount of joy and frustration. Let me not get into software experiments, finding alternatives which was always a mixed bag of experiences. And then few months or weeks later, starting it over with another Linux version with slightly more advanced capabilities. There was rapid innovation that I lived through, and these struggles started disappearing as Linux installs became more and more easier and mainstream — there was a Linux boot loader CD that even let the OS run from CD, (then there was USB version of the same), followed by even Linux installers from Windows version and then later, of course, it was mainstream with some laptops that came with a Linux OS installed. Somewhere around that time I stopped tinkering with Linux — partly because I was tired already, partly because I was frustrated, partly because it wasn’t that exciting/adventurous/achievement anymore — but mostly because it wasn’t “cool” as it was already mainstream.

Coming to 3rd leg of my story — I resisted buying my smartphone until I finished my MBA — during which I noticed that iPhone was already very mainstream. It was a no-tinkering, hard walled ecosystem that was used by anyone’s mom, to teenager to surprisingly, many geeks too. It was MS Windows of my computer age. People were fans because they wanted to be seen as cool, without realizing that it was no longer the ‘cool’ brand technically, but it didn’t matter to fanboys. Not only that, Android partly based on Linux and the attached open source mentality, was starting to take off and was a real challenger to iPhone. Android was open for third party integrations/key boards, there was file system access, and viola- entire OS could be replaced ( flashing a custom ROM as it became popularly known as). There was, similar to RedHats and Ubuntus, Cyanogenmods in Android world. It was a great time to be a ‘rebel’ again (ironically, Apple still captured the ‘rebel’ ‘pirate’ ‘cool’ brand long after Jobs and Apple was that, and when they were more mainstream than anyone else before. Now, Apple is similar to IBM which they fought early in their existence). That’s the promise and attraction that led me to make my first smart phones as Android (it also helped that I managed products on Android platform at my job — I became ‘the Android guy’).

Over the last 4 years or so, Android has become more mainstream — it is not even ‘Android’ but just one smartphone manufacturer that pretty much dominates the market. The difference between the OS and differences in features themselves are very minimal I think and each developer conference, both the companies act as if they have launched the first revolutionary smartphone. Once the experimentation and excitement wears off, the frustrations start bothering. Be it in battery life, camera quality/speed, and many other small things, minor irritants on Android started bothering me. Some good products like Android Pay were blocked by Verizon or Samsung. Google didn’t have wherewithal or execution expertise to launch something, build an ecosystem and to bring all partners on board. Similar story with Android Wear — some of the user experiences ended up ‘getting influenced’ by Apple once Apple Watch launched. All the new applications were solely developed for iPhone (although I used to joke that, developers are experimenting on iOS, and when they are successful they will join Android..which means there are only successful apps on Android, it was rarely true) and as I am an early adopter it bothered me a lot that I can’t test or use certain applications. The problem was visible very clearly once Product Hunt launched- so much so that I stopped visiting Product Hunt to minimize my agony.

I have now switched jobs, hardly have time or interest to visit Product Hunt, any phone is OK to use and is not very relevant to my work and I am tired of experimenting or have any toleration for frustration in phone experience. So it doesn’t matter any more, but as I am buying new phones and I am switching over to iPhone. I know what I am going to miss, and I know what I’ll gain.

When I think about all this, the reason I was ‘Android guy’ and the reason I am switching to ‘iPhone’ is mostly about who I am. Those platforms didn’t attract me because of what they were, but I was attracted to them because of who I was at that point of time.
