It was not about just a web app

July 1st 2013. A date most would remember as the last day of Google Reader. I haven’t seen as passionate loyalty and following towards this product as even hatred against any other. Companies, start ups and product managers would give their right hand to get a fraction of such support and love from community.

Yet, Google decided to axe this product. But not before attempting to trample it before. Last year when sharing and following (‘social’) were removed from the interface. That drastic redesign (“ohh all white space that some designers loved for the sake of love towards white space”), and killing ‘Bookmarklet’. That’s when I stopped using Google Reader. I saw it coming. Reader was on lifeline, on death bed, staring at you fondly but unable to wake up and walk, and scared in eyes. It was just about time that they announced it dead. And finally when it was announced, I wasn’t as shocked.

But it is not about a product - a product no mainstream people understood or ‘got’. ‘RSS’ a scary acronym didn’t help either. But it helped productivity immensely. And made discovering content, sharing content so much easier. It didn’t have ‘I had this for breakfast’ and an accompanied ‘Instagram’ picture. It had best content, shared by your trusted people. It had news, blogs, and private posts. Bookmarklet allowed you to grab a slice of internet and make it your own.

Sharing, starring was one click. You didn’t ‘have’ to add your bytes. Almost the design and interface never came in the way of consuming content.

All my reading happened on this platform - ALL. If a page didn’t support RSS, I probably never visited it 2nd time. Reader was my home page of the browser, it was default page I would open on a new tab. I used it, trust me here, more than ‘gmail’. When company blocked access to gmail and general internet including social networking, Reader was still present. (probably hiding from IT’s filter list).

Of course it all didn’t begin this great. I was a ‘Bloglines’ user then. First versions of Reader was so shabby and complicated to use that I went back to Bloglines after about 10 minutes. I thought I might never return but they listened to our feedback and released a complete redesign. I came back, awed and never left again.

Until they kicked me out with a white space redesign that killed bookmarklet.

Once Yahoo started charging for a mail feature (or storage I forget now) that was free until then,I left Yahoo in search of other mail clients. I was on gmx.net and several others who gave “GB” space for free until I chanced upon Gmail. I never went back to Yahoo despite their numerous redesigns and changes in storage space.

Google killed my another favorite product “Notebooks”. Now Google has kicked me out by killing Reader. I look at Google now very scared thinking what they will sunset next.

It is not about one product - it is about memories, people, knowledge, information and finally about trust.
